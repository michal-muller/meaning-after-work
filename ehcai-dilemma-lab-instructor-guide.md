# EHCAI Dilemma Lab — Instructor Guide

## Purpose

EHCAI Dilemma Lab is a classroom simulation for management students. It helps students explore how artificial intelligence changes managerial decision-making, responsibility, human judgment, care, visibility and organizational ethics.

The simulation is not about whether AI is simply good or bad. It asks a more difficult management question:

**What becomes visible, what becomes invisible, and who remains responsible when AI enters organizational decision-making?**

## Core learning goals

After the activity, students should be able to:

1. Distinguish between AI as a signal and AI as a decision-maker.
2. Identify what an AI system makes visible and what it may obscure.
3. Analyse responsibility across multiple organizational actors.
4. Recognize the limits of data-driven management.
5. Develop a human-centred managerial response to AI-supported recommendations.
6. Explain why responsible AI use requires interpretation, not only technical adoption.
7. Reflect on the relationship between efficiency, care, accountability and meaningful work.

## Recommended use

The simulation can be used in courses on:

* Management
* Organizational behaviour
* Human resource management
* Leadership
* Business ethics
* Digital transformation
* AI in organizations
* Human-centred management
* Responsible innovation
* Strategic management

## Recommended group size

Students should work in groups of 4–6.

Each group may either:

* work on the same case as the whole class, or
* work on different cases and compare results at the end.

For larger classes, assign the same case to multiple groups and compare differences in responsibility mapping and final decisions.

## Recommended duration

### Short version: 30–45 minutes

* 5 min: Introduction
* 10–15 min: Evidence rounds
* 10 min: Decision and responsibility map
* 10–15 min: Debrief

### Standard seminar version: 60–90 minutes

* 10 min: Introduction
* 10 min: Role assignment
* 20–25 min: Evidence rounds
* 15 min: Decision board and responsibility map
* 15 min: Decision memo
* 15–20 min: Class debrief

### Extended workshop version: 120 minutes

* 10 min: Introduction
* 15 min: Role assignment and first decision
* 30 min: Evidence rounds
* 20 min: Responsibility mapping
* 20 min: Decision memo
* 25 min: Cross-case comparison and debrief

## Instructor preparation

Before class:

1. Open the simulation page and test the language switch.
2. Decide whether all groups will work on one case or different cases.
3. Prepare the student worksheet.
4. Decide whether students will submit the final memo.
5. Clarify that students should not include personal, medical, workplace or third-party sensitive information in their responses.
6. Emphasize that the cases are fictional teaching scenarios.

## Opening explanation for students

You may introduce the activity as follows:

> Today we will work with AI-supported management dilemmas. The goal is not to decide whether AI is good or bad. The goal is to understand how AI changes what managers notice, what they ignore, and who becomes responsible. Each case gives you an AI-supported recommendation and then gradually reveals additional evidence. Your task is to make a responsible management decision and justify it.

## Core EHCAI framing

Use this framing before the simulation:

EHCAI means Existential Human-Centred AI. It asks how intelligent systems affect human agency, responsibility, meaning and co-being in organizations.

In management, AI does not only automate tasks. It also changes:

* what counts as evidence
* what becomes visible
* what becomes invisible
* who is trusted
* who is blamed
* who is heard
* who disappears from the decision

The key question is:

**How can managers use AI without abandoning human responsibility?**

## Suggested classroom flow

### Step 1: First impression

Ask students to read only the case brief and core dilemma.

Ask:

* What would you decide immediately?
* What information seems most important?
* What assumption are you making?

Do not let them finalize the decision yet.

### Step 2: Role assignment

Assign roles within each group.

Students should speak from their role perspective, not only from personal opinion.

Possible prompt:

> Your role does not determine your final position, but it gives you a responsibility to notice something others may miss.

### Step 3: Evidence rounds

Students reveal evidence one round at a time.

After each round, ask them to revise:

* What changed?
* What became more complicated?
* What did the AI system not capture?
* Would your decision change?

Recommended rule:

Students must write a provisional decision after Round 1 and revise it after each new round.

### Step 4: Decision board

Students select one decision from the decision board.

They may choose one of the provided decisions or use it as a basis for a more nuanced answer in their memo.

Ask:

* What are the strengths of this decision?
* Who benefits from it?
* Who may be harmed by it?
* What does it assume about AI?
* What does it assume about human responsibility?

### Step 5: Responsibility map

Students click actors in the responsibility map.

They should assign:

* primary responsibility
* shared responsibility
* no direct responsibility

Important: the goal is not to find one guilty person. The goal is to understand how responsibility is distributed.

Ask:

* Who produced the AI output?
* Who interprets it?
* Who acts on it?
* Who is affected by it?
* Who can stop or revise the decision?
* Who benefits from ambiguity?

### Step 6: Decision memo

Students complete the EHCAI decision memo.

Required sections:

* What the AI made visible
* What the AI made invisible
* Responsibility chain
* Human-centred intervention
* Risks of the decision
* What a responsible manager should do next

The memo should be short, concrete and defensible.

### Step 7: Debrief

Use the case-specific debrief questions and the general questions below.

## General debrief questions

Use after any case:

1. What did the AI system make easier to see?
2. What did the AI system make harder to see?
3. Did the AI output create clarity or false certainty?
4. Who became more powerful because of the AI system?
5. Who became more vulnerable?
6. Where did responsibility almost disappear?
7. What would happen if the organization followed the AI output without human review?
8. What would happen if the organization rejected AI entirely?
9. What does a human-centred manager need to notice?
10. What would you change in the organization’s AI governance?

## Instructor notes on responsibility

Students often first look for one actor who is responsible.

Encourage them to see responsibility as distributed:

* The vendor is responsible for system design and documentation.
* Managers are responsible for interpretation and action.
* HR is responsible for policy and fairness.
* Senior leadership is responsible for organizational pressure and incentives.
* The organization is responsible for governance.
* Employees are affected by decisions but should not be blamed for system limitations.

Key teaching point:

**AI does not remove responsibility. It redistributes it and may make it harder to see.**

## Instructor notes on human-centred decision-making

A human-centred decision is not necessarily slow, soft or anti-technology.

A human-centred decision:

* treats AI as support, not replacement
* asks what context is missing
* considers affected people
* protects responsibility
* documents reasoning
* avoids automatic escalation
* distinguishes signal from judgment
* preserves the possibility of appeal, dialogue and revision

## Common student mistakes

### Mistake 1: Treating the AI output as objective truth

Response:

Ask what data the system had and what data it did not have.

### Mistake 2: Rejecting AI too quickly

Response:

Ask what the system correctly noticed and how that signal could still be useful.

### Mistake 3: Looking only for legal compliance

Response:

Ask whether a decision can be legally defensible and still humanly irresponsible.

### Mistake 4: Turning care into paternalism

Response:

Ask whether the affected person has voice, consent and agency.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring organizational incentives

Response:

Ask who benefits if the AI output is followed quickly.

## Assessment option

The final memo may be graded using the following criteria.

### 1. Understanding of the AI signal

Does the group clearly identify what the AI system made visible?

### 2. Recognition of missing context

Does the group identify what the AI system failed to see?

### 3. Responsibility mapping

Does the group show distributed responsibility rather than blaming only one actor?

### 4. Quality of human-centred intervention

Is the proposed intervention concrete, realistic and ethically defensible?

### 5. Management reasoning

Does the group connect the decision to organizational consequences?

### 6. Reflection

Does the group articulate a broader EHCAI insight?

## Suggested grading rubric

### Excellent

The memo clearly distinguishes AI signal from managerial judgment, identifies missing human context, maps responsibility across actors, proposes a concrete human-centred intervention and reflects on broader organizational implications.

### Good

The memo identifies the main dilemma and proposes a reasonable decision, but the responsibility map or human-centred intervention is less developed.

### Satisfactory

The memo gives a decision and some justification, but treats the AI output too simply or misses important human context.

### Weak

The memo accepts or rejects AI without analysis, does not identify responsibility and does not propose a concrete management response.

## Ethical and data note

This simulation is for educational purposes.

It is not a psychological, legal, clinical or workplace assessment.

Students should not enter sensitive personal, medical, workplace or third-party information.

If student reflections are later used for research, a separate informed consent process is required.

## Possible follow-up assignments

### Short reflection

Write 500 words on the question:

**What did the AI system make invisible, and why does this matter for management?**

### Comparative reflection

Compare two cases and answer:

**How does responsibility move differently across the two situations?**

### Policy memo

Write a one-page organizational policy for responsible AI-supported management decisions.

### Personal reflection

Describe one managerial decision where data would be useful but insufficient.

Students should not use real confidential workplace cases unless anonymized and approved.

## Suggested closing statement

End the session with:

> Human-centred AI in management is not about rejecting systems. It is about refusing to let systems become the place where responsibility disappears.
